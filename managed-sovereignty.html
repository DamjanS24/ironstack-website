Your systems, cared for.
Still yours.

I · THE PROBLEM

“Self-hosting sounds great, until something breaks”

Most companies do not stay on rented cloud because they love it. They stay because someone else carries the pager. The honest fear behind “we could never self-host” is rarely about features; it is the 3 a.m. question: who patches this, who notices when the disk fills up, who restores the backup, and does the backup even restore?

That fear is legitimate. A server nobody maintains is worse than a subscription. Owned infrastructure without care is not sovereignty, it is neglect with extra steps.

Managed Sovereignty exists to close exactly that gap: you get the ownership, and I carry the care. Not as a favor bolted onto a project, but as the actual product, with defined response windows and a report every month that tells you what happened in plain language.

II · WHAT IT IS, CONCRETELY

Custodianship with a fixed price

Managed Sovereignty is a monthly agreement per tier. The tiers differ in response windows and included hours, not in whether your systems are looked after; every environment I run gets the same discipline, because my monitoring does not know which tier you are on.

III · A FAIR MATCH

Who this is for, and who it is not for

FOR YOU IF
  • You want to own your systems but nobody on your team wants to be the sysadmin.
  • You value one accountable person over an anonymous support queue.
  • You want to know, every month, what your infrastructure did and what it cost.
  • You want the option to leave, every month, without a penalty clause.
NOT FOR YOU IF
  • You need a contractual 24/7 operations center with sub-hour response at any hour. I am one person and will not pretend otherwise.
  • Your environment must stay on a US hyperscaler for reasons that are real and yours. Then this whole company is the wrong fit, and I will say so on the first call.
  • You want the cheapest possible hosting. Care costs money; neglect costs more, but later.

IV · WHERE IT SITS

Every path ends here

Managed Sovereignty is the heart of Ironstack; the other services are how you arrive. The Audit tells you what you have and what it costs. The Stack builds fresh; Migration moves an existing environment, both through the same five-phase method. Phase five is literally called Handover & Retainer: the moment the build ends, this service begins. AI components live under the same roof, at €75 per month each on any tier.

You can also arrive sideways. Some clients start with a single automation project and grow into a stack; some bring an environment someone else built. The entry matters less than the destination: systems you own, running with care.

Fair questions

What exactly is included in the monthly price?

Every tier covers updates and security patching, backups with tested restores, monitoring, certificate renewals, and a monthly health report in plain language. Base, at €150, is care only, with response the next business day. Standard, at €325, adds two hours of changes and questions each month, with response within four business hours. Priority, at €575, adds five hours, same-day response, and a quarterly review call. All excl. btw.

What happens if the server goes down at night?

Monitoring never sleeps and alerts me before most people notice anything. Each tier has an honestly defined response window, agreed in writing. I will not promise a 24/7 operations center, because I am one person; I promise a defined window and a documented recovery procedure that works without heroics.

Can we do maintenance ourselves instead?

Yes. Everything is documented for your team, not for me, so you can take over any month. Some clients run day-to-day themselves and keep a smaller tier for escalations. Both are fine; the documentation is the same either way.

Does the price go up once we depend on you?

No. The monthly price is fixed per tier and agreed before anything starts. It changes only if you change tier or the environment materially grows, and then only with a new fixed number you approve first. And you never depend on me: the exit clause applies every single month.

What does the monthly health report contain?

What was updated, what the backups did and whether restores were tested, uptime, anything that needs a decision from you, and what I recommend next. Written in plain language, not log excerpts.

We did not build our systems with Ironstack. Can you still run them?

Usually yes, after an audit. I need to know what is running before I take responsibility for it, and the audit is exactly that investigation. If your environment is something I cannot honestly care for, I will say so.
